The core idea

One threshold, four policies

Flag every row whose volume exceeds a single cutoff, and the same rule catches a tenth of one channel and two thirds of another. Drag the threshold and watch a shared number stop being a shared policy.

2Why this happens

Each curve is a survival function, $S_g(t)=\Pr(\text{volume} > t \mid \text{channel } g)$, the share of a channel that a cutoff of $t$ would flag. The channels sit at very different levels, so a vertical line at one value slices each curve at a different height. A fixed cutoff is really four different flag rates wearing one number. Set it at 3 and read the table: it is the 90th percentile of one channel and the 30th of another.

3What to do instead

Flag inside each channel, not across them. Either use each channel's own percentile, so "top 5%" means the same thing everywhere, or choose a cutoff per channel from the cost of a false flag. The threshold becomes a policy again once it is conditional on the channel. A single global number cannot be, no matter how carefully it is chosen.
