Five modules, each in two halves: the math worked by hand with a pencil, and the same idea run for real in R with verified output. The running example throughout is a skewed, multi-channel volume score, so the methods stay attached to a real applied problem instead of floating free as formulas.

Applied deep dives
The core idea

One threshold, four policies

A single cutoff flags a tenth of one channel and two thirds of another. Drag the threshold and watch a shared rule fall apart across channels.

 Extremeness

How extreme is a value?

The answer is its percentile, and it depends entirely on the channel. A value of 3 is the 90th percentile in one and the 30th in another.

 Comparing two groups

Regression, Welch, and the p-value that lies

b1 is the difference in means. The default significance test beside it is a foregone conclusion at large n, and its standard error assumes equal variance.

 Diagnostics

How to read a QQ plot

Toggle the shape of a distribution and watch the QQ plot bend. It is a shape check, not a way to locate where a single value sits.

What's built, what's next

The five modules above plus the four applied pages and the practice bank are complete and checked. This guide keeps growing. Planned next, roughly in order:

  • Multiple regression in depth. Omitted-variable bias made concrete, interaction terms, and reading a real regression table end to end.
  • Model diagnostics. Residual plots, testing for heteroskedasticity, and what to do when the Gauss-Markov conditions fail.
  • Quantile regression. The natural next step after Module 5, and the tool this site's own threshold problem actually calls for.
  • Causal inference. Difference-in-differences and instrumental variables, for when a coefficient needs to earn a causal claim.
  • Maximum likelihood & logistic regression. The estimation principle underneath most of what came before, made explicit.
